**Summary of "Transformers Can Overcome the Curse of Dimensionality: A Theoretical Study from an Approximation Perspective"**

This paper presents a theoretical investigation into the expressive power of Transformer models, specifically their ability to approximate complex functions and address the "curse of dimensionality."

### 1. Key Concepts

*   **Transformer Model:** A widely used deep learning architecture (especially in NLP) characterized by self-attention mechanisms and feedforward layers.
*   **Curse of Dimensionality:** A phenomenon where the difficulty of problems (e.g., approximation, optimization) increases exponentially with the dimensionality of the data or function space. The paper demonstrates how Transformers can *overcome* this.
*   **Function Approximation Theory:** The mathematical study of how well a given class of functions (e.g., neural networks) can approximate other functions.
*   **H¨older Continuous Function Class:** A specific class of "smooth" functions that the Transformers in this study are shown to approximate effectively.
*   **Self-Attention Layer:** A core component of Transformers that allows the model to weigh the importance of different parts of the input sequence relative to each other.
*   **Feedforward Layers:** Standard dense neural network layers used within the Transformer architecture, often with activation functions like ReLU, floor, or others.
*   **Kolmogorov-Arnold Superposition Theorem:** A fundamental theorem in mathematics that provides a basis for the construction of the Transformers in this paper, enabling their approximation capabilities without relying on contextual mapping.
*   **Contextual Mapping:** A concept used in *previous* theoretical studies of Transformer approximation (e.g., Yun et al.) to aggregate dependencies. This paper's novel approach *avoids* contextual mapping, leading to a more intuitive proof.
*   **Approximation Accuracy ($\epsilon$):** A measure of how close the Transformer's output is to the true function. The paper analyzes the required network size (layers, width) to achieve a certain $\epsilon$.
